Design at Scale
Accelerating product delivery with a full-stack Design System @ Equinix | 2021 - Sr. UX Design Manager
Summary:

I founded Quix, Equinix’s first company-wide design system, streamlining design, reducing technical debt, and improving usability. It also established a design-at-scale culture, ensuring consistency and efficiency across teams.

Quix is now the foundation of UX at Equinix, driving efficiency, collaboration, and scalable design excellence across teams.​​​​​​​
My Role:

• Built Quix from scratch, strategy, principles, and adoption.
• Partnered with design, product, and engineering to scale reusable components.
• Established governance & contribution models for long-term growth.
• Launched a self-serve documentation hub for easy adoption.
• Championed a design-at-scale culture, embedding system thinking across teams.
• Influenced senior executives to secure buy-in, funding, and build the design team.

Impact:
💰 Saved $1.5M in 6 months by cutting redundant design & dev work.
⏩ Cut development time by 40%, speeding up feature releases.
🎨 Increased design consistency by 60%, improving usability.
🛠️ Reduced UI engineering effort by 30%, freeing teams for innovation.
📈 Adopted by 10+ product teams, making Quix a company-wide standard.

My journey started at Equinix as an entrepreneur, where I applied my business learnings from working closely with Joyti Bansal and Rishi Singh at Harness. I founded a company-wide design system (we call it Quix), which was also instrumental in building a centralised UX Tribe and "Design at Scale" culture to expedite product delivery.
The First 90 Days
At Equinix, I started as a Principal UX Designer - an influential strategic leader role. My previous technology and business knowledge was an asset, but the challenge was navigating the right people within a large organization and gaining institutional knowledge for this new role.​​​​​​​ What I needed was a structured framework to learn and move fast. The First 90 Days, a book by Michael Watkins, helped diagnose my situation, define the core challenges, and design plans to create momentum, resulting in a more successful and comfortable transition. I focused on four pillars of effective onboarding - business orientation, stakeholder connection, alignment of expectations, and cultural adaptation. My user research skill sets allowed me to meet stakeholders, ask questions, and analyze. Next was the time for me to paint and present the Big Picture with a data-driven strategic story. The main pain points for customers were too many portals, inconsistent UX, and limited self-serve capabilities. On the other hand, business go-to-market velocity was slower than desired. Product scalability and ongoing costs to maintain and evolve various portals were a big issue. How do we solve these issues?

Our Head of UX, Michael Kopcsak, also joined simultaneously and was challenged to build a solid foundation for a centralized UX function. He helped me present my ideas at SVP to the entire UX org level. He also helped us frame our thinking in 3Cs—Converged, Consistent, and Coherent. The design system was built with consistent and coherent principles.
Business Case
A design system combines UX language, tools, technology, process, and people — and people are essential. The biggest challenges I faced in this initiative were culture change, trust, and commitment to design, build, and adopt the system. As this initiative unfolded, we developed an organization-wide shared language about building products that significantly improved our PM, UX, and engineering efficiency.
Like all other business initiatives, it was necessary to pitch a good business story threaded with problem space, target market/users, solution, business model, success—early traction and milestones, adoption strategy, proposed team, and ROI (return on investment). It was essential to build a business case with solid data points like current product development cost and market trend analysis, build vs. buy, technology stack, and more. I needed to have a concrete foundational thought before I pitched to SVPs.

I was challenged to build a company-wide Design System - where the culture was for product teams to work in silo mode. Hence, there is a massive in-consistency from UX to front-end to the backend to operation. So, the design system is an opportunity for organizational mindset change. It was essential to map the influential stakeholders and align with them during the foundational setup process. Let's take a brief dive into a few crucial foundational thoughts.

Principles: I first started with a few setup assumptions, which I reviewed with key stakeholders and set the fundamental principles for Equinix Design System. Such as the Design System -
Value Proposition: Like other product inception, it was necessary to frame the value propositions with customers.
MVP Strategy: As the definition of a Design System, we first think of it as a collection of reusable components, guided by clear standards, that can be assembled to build any number of applications to solve customer problems. However, it takes time to convince the product team to adopt the new system component by component when we think about the adoption strategy. The adoption strategy needs to bring direct value to the end customer's experience enhancement, business impact, and revenue increase - for example; most Equinix products were transactional - a common user motion but inconsistent product-by-product experiences. I took a top-down value delivery approach to define the MVP scope.
Build vs. Buy: At Equinix, all these product teams used different front-end technology stacks over the years. Equinix was also acquiring digital software companies. How can they quickly adopt and integrate design system components? We consciously decided to take a framework-agnostic approach and build our design system using Lit Web components.
Team Operational Model
When it comes to design systems, we often talk about tools and methodologies to begin and maintain them. Still, above all, the human beings behind it are what guarantee longevity and internal adoption. Now, more designers code, developers design, and product managers have their hands dirty with everything. They all work tribally in teams spread throughout an enterprise. You can't legitimately tell them what to do anymore. You have to work together to build something bigger and more cohesive.

So, how do you form a team and operational model that best helps you stay cohesive with a system?
It was time for me to research and understand how others solve this problem at large-scale organizations. I interviewed Material Design, Fluent, Carbon Design System, and AWS folks to gather market data on the design system's operational process, governance, and adoption approach. 
Nathan Curtis has written a lot about Design Systems. In one of his posts, he goes over three ways companies organize their teams for their design systems today: the solitary, centralized, and federated models. The solitary model is the most fragile because it has a built-in risk — overlords don't scale. Many teams are moving away from the solitary model to the centralized or federated model — those models are usually much better for scaling the design system.
We decided to take a combination of the centralized and federated models. While building a core design system team, we will also have contributors who act as a federation of practitioners from many products and platform areas.
Building Design System Core Team
At Equinix, we work with US, Poland, and Singapore global teams who will adopt and work with the core team members to build and scale this system. Internally these silo teams had their own regional cultures and operational characteristics. During the foundational research process, I realized inclusive and collaborative approach would be essential energy suppliers for building a shared language. I ran design thinking workshops with global teams (UX folks and engineers) to define the Design System Core team member's competencies.
Takeaways from those sessions - the core team is the control tower that will provide the cross-functional inclusive vision necessary to guarantee the overall coherence of the product experience. Other aspects that must be considered to contribute to its success include promotion for adoption, establishing processes for maintenance, updates, and communication.

I formed a core team with multidisciplinary skill sets - four UX designers, five engineers, a front-end architect, an engineering team leader, a UX content writer, and a UX researcher. I hired folks who are, in general, broad generalists but have deep discipline expertise in one or two areas. During the hiring process, I looked for a few essential competencies - System Thinker, customer and user-focused, an eye for minimal visual design, strong communication, passion for design, operation, and efficiency, an urge to help designers/engineers, self-awareness, and curiosity.
So, what was my role? As a founder, at the beginning of the journey, you like to keep your team small enough to be more agile and deliver fast to create momentum, which requires wearing multiple hats as a leader, rallying the team, and being hands-on at the same time. I wore various hats to deliver Quix MVP with my core team and in partnership with contributors across the global engineering, UX, and Equinix Brand team.
Design and Engineering Efficiency
At Harness, I designed CI/CD and Cloud Cost Management tools to empower developers, which is the modern engineering norm. Equinix was just in the process of moving in that direction. Rapid development is demanded of developers, with regular feedback from the customer and stakeholders that needs to be tackled faster, which gives rise to the problem of Developer Efficiency. Designers and developers need to maintain quality without hindering speed. During our initial audit, we realized we had incurred tremendous UX and tech debt over the years by introducing and integrating several different component libraries across our product lines. It was time to streamline tools, processes, and operations across all three - design, development, and content.
The 3Gs - A Friendly Governance Culture
Do you cringe when you hear the word "governance?" We don't like rules as creatives, and being told what to do is tough. Regarding design systems, governance is a framework for clarifying roles, responsibilities, and authority over decisions. Having that clarity ensures that decisions for the design system funnel smoothly through the governance process. One takeaway from the initial design thinking workshop with the global teams was that we need a structured but friendly governance process aligned with Equinix's value "We belong!" - what would lead us to a successful unified design system and that single source of truth we sought. This is where we proposed the 3Gs - Guide, Guru, and Guild.
Scaling Design System with Collaboration and Contribution
It's essential to have a good support plan for any successful product. attention to from the beginning. How do you scale your system with a small core team? We needed to have a structured collaboration and contribution model that sets the culture of inclusiveness and boosts the adoption and growth of the system. We planned for three general categories of requests that any design system user could make - report a bug, request something new, and provide general feedback or suggestions.

We designed a process with a contribution checklist for adding a new component/pattern. There could be two possible scenarios - 

DS Backlog - Submit a Jira request for the design system backlog when the product team has no resources to contribute. The core team will review, prioritize, and allocate resources based on bandwidth. 

Contribution Checklist - If the product team has a new component/pattern request and resources to develop, follow the guidelines and contribute to the design system.
Inclusive and Transparent Culture
Trust is the backbone of an open and inclusive culture, and the right processes—govern how you design, develop, and work together as a team. The biggest challenges I faced in this initiative were culture change, trust, and commitment to design, build, and adopt the system. Early on, I defined the OKRs of these initiatives, and one of them was building an inclusive and transparent culture - which I also cascaded to the design system core team's goals. I took several actions to achieve this OKR with my team, which mainly fall into three categories - inclusion, communication, and evangelization.

You can calculate cost savings in several ways - our business case is already showcasing a minimal formula around time, effort, and cost. Lessons & Growth as a Design Leader​​​​​​​
Business: The Intersection of Strategy, Design, Technology & Business
I thrive at the intersection of strategy, design, technology, and business because good design is good business. In my first 90 days of observing the landscape, we realized we needed to drive significant strategic experience changes. However, shifting an organization’s culture requires a phased approach, ensuring alignment and adoption at scale.

At the heart of my work was empowering teams and individuals to build a creative, innovative, scalable, and impactful foundation for business success. This meant boldly challenging the status quo while influencing change at a global organizational level. Throughout this journey, I learned to embrace critical feedback—from executives to ICs—and turn skeptics into champions of design-led transformation.

People: Building Relationships & Fostering Growth
What I love most about UX is staying close to people. Meaningful relationships with colleagues and stakeholders are essential to delivering impactful design solutions. I used strategic design, design thinking, and creative facilitation to bring people together and co-create in a psychologically safe space.

This experience also pushed me to grow as a people leader—building trust, investing in my team’s growth, and motivating them to make a meaningful impact. I realized that unlocking creativity and potential isn’t just about learning new skills but also about removing limiting factors—both internal (self-doubt) and external (organizational barriers). Psychological safety and inclusion stood out as key areas where we could foster a culture of belonging, embodying Equinix’s core value: “We Belong.”

Scaling Design at a Global Level
Leading Quix was a large-scale initiative. I collaborated with 50+ UX professionals, hundreds of engineers, and senior directors across the organisation. This scale of impact meant learning from every conversation, challenge, and success. One of the most significant influences on my journey was my leader, Michael Kpocsak. His leadership, both in moments of triumph and challenge, became a source of deep learning for me.

This experience solidified my belief in design as a business driver and the power of people-first leadership. If you’d like to chat more about design, business, or leadership, feel free to reach out. I’d love to connect!
